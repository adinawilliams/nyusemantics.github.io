Only and association with negative antonyms

Sam Alxatib, CUNY

Abstract

A problem is detected in how only, traditionally viewed, associates with Negative Antonyms (NAs) like (very) few, and rarely, e.g. (1).

    1. John only attended [very few]F meetings.
    2. John only rarelyF visits his grandmother.

The predicted meanings, which negate the stronger alternatives of the relevant NAs, is shown to be incorrect. The attested meanings are shown to be truth-conditionally equivalent to the result of removing only, hence giving the illusion that only is vacuous. It is further observed that, given an NA, sentences where only appears to be vacuous become ungrammatical when the NA is replaced with its positive counterpart, e.g.

    1. ?? John only attended [very many]F meetings.
    2. ?? John only frequentlyF visits his grandmother.

I argue for a view that relates the two phenomena: the ungrammaticality of the only-positive cases (2) explains the unavailability of the predicted meanings in only-negative cases (1). The attested readings for the negatives, where only appears to be vacuous, is derived from LFs that feature a silent existential quantifier above the NA. Though the approach is shown to have technical shortcomings, it correctly accounts for the behavioral difference between NAs and their positive counterparts.
